WATER AVAILABILITY

A broad topic, encompassing the biophysical supply of water, the demand for water, and access to water. Water cycles through the earth system, precipitating from the atmosphere and becoming available as surface water and groundwater. The papers in the initial sections define water fluxes and storage and describe methods for quantifying them. Most analyses of water availability rely on hydrologic models, so included is a standalone section devoted to papers discussing the ways in which models differ and what they are most useful for. Models are useful for quantification and prediction, but the water cycle is variable. Drought describes periods when water availability is limited, and papers in this section define and identify drought in a variety of ways. Unpredictability and changes in water availability will increase in an era of global change. The effects of land-use change and of changing climate are explored both conceptually and through quantitative modeling. The preceding sections address the presence of water, but presence does not guarantee availability. The following sections address water availability. To state that water is available implies that it is available for ecosystems and human societies to use. The section on water use addresses sources of water and demand for water in agriculture, in households and industry, and for nature. The physical presence of water also does not ensure that it is available to those who need it. Water accessibility is related to physical factors such as infrastructure and also to the political context of governance. When demand for water is unmet, people experience water scarcity. Water scarcity can be quantified in a variety of ways, and potential solutions to scarcity are often a function of how it is defined. Even though there are many elements to water availability, it is clear that it cannot be understood without an integrated, interdisciplinary assessment of both biophysical and social factors.

Key Points And Importance

  • Global Water Resources:
    • Earth's surface is covered by about 71% water, but the vast majority (approximately 97%) is saltwater found in oceans and seas, which is not suitable for most human uses.
    • Only a small fraction (around 2.5%) is freshwater, and the remaining 0.5% is locked in glaciers and polar ice caps.

  • Properties of Water:
    • Water is one of the most important resources on the planet. Without water, life cannot exist. But what makes water so important? Water has several unique characteristics that make it an extremely valuable resource. Some such properties of water are listed below:
    • Water is a very good solvent – it has the ability to dissolve many substances.
    • The boiling point and freezing point of water make it easily available in all three states (solid, liquid, and gaseous).
    • The specific heat of water is quite high. This enables water to absorb and release heat slowly, thereby regulating the temperature of its environment.
    • Owing to its transparency, water can allow light to reach the life forms that are submerged in it. This is crucial for the survival of plant life in the oceans, lakes, and rivers.
    • Water is neither acidic nor basic in nature. It has a pH of 7, making it a neutral substance.
  • Importance:
    • These unique qualities of water, along with its abundance on the planet (approximately 71% of the Earth’s surface is made up of water), make it a crucial resource for plants, animals, and human beings.

Global Water Availability

People have a tremendous impact on water availability. Through direct management, including damming rivers and cultivating the land surface, and indirectly, mostly pressingly by changing the earth’s climate, people have altered the amount of water available to them. This new era, in which people affect the earth as much as or more than earth-system processes do, has been called the Anthropocene. Meybeck 2003 introduces the idea of the Anthropocene specifically in the context of water. Vorosmarty, et al. 2000 elaborates on the human factors that affect the water cycle, and Haddeland, et al. 2014 provides a quantitative estimate of the magnitude of the direct and indirect pathways of influence. Not all regions of the world will be affected in the same way by changes in land use and climate. Alcamo and Henrichs 2002 identifies the ~10 percent of global land area that is likely to be heavily affected by water shortage due to global change, and Lehner, et al. 2006 evaluates the likelihood of increased flooding at smaller scales. Milly, et al. 2008 argues that the central paradigm of water resources management, which is based on the idea that knowledge of past water flow allows prediction of future water flow, is no longer accurate in the Anthropocene and that this paradigm must change.

Groundwater

Groundwater is a key water source for both agricultural and household use because it can often be accessed though a well, obviating the need for substantial infrastructure, and because aquifers serve as storage so water can be tapped “on demand” when needed. Yet, because it is out of sight, quantifying available groundwater resources is difficult. Zektser and Everett 2004 provides an overview of groundwater availability and use globally and details the ways groundwater is measured and characterized. Strassberg, et al. 2009 documents changes in groundwater by using the GRACE satellite. In addition to explaining the technology, this article is particularly useful because it compares satellite measurements to ground-based measurements. Zhou 2009 tackles the question of how much and how fast water ought to be withdrawn from an aquifer, reviewing arguments about drawdown, recharge, and discharge. One reason evaluating safe or sustainable groundwater use is difficult is that groundwater is not separate from surface water, though they are often evaluated and managed separately. Döll, et al. 2012 focuses on interconnections between ground and surface water and evaluates how groundwater use for irrigation, household use, and manufacturing can affect surface flows, and vice versa. Siebert, et al. 2010 focuses explicitly on groundwater use for agriculture; in presenting their findings they provide a useful discussion of why it is difficult to track groundwater use. Groundwater recharge occurs at different rates in different aquifers, and some groundwater is effectively nonrenewable. Foster and Chilton 2003 explores how different types of groundwater might be used sustainably. Steward, et al. 2013 evaluates optimal ways to use groundwater when it is essentially a fixed resource.

Factors Affecting Water Availability

Several factors influence water availability, including climate, geography, population density, water management practices, and pollution levels. Areas with low rainfall, such as deserts, are naturally water-scarce, while regions with abundant rainfall and well-managed water resources tend to have better water availability.

Water Management

Effective water management is crucial for ensuring water availability. This includes the responsible use of water resources, efficient irrigation practices in agriculture, sustainable groundwater management, and measures to reduce water pollution.

Sustainable Development Goal (SDG)

The United Nations has included clean water and sanitation as one of the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) to be achieved by 2030. The aim is to ensure availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all.

Sources of water

  • The following are the different water sources:
    • Rainwater: The primary source of water is rain. It is considered to be the purest form of natural water available. The rains also carry the smoke and dust particles into the atmosphere. As a result, the initial rain shower has a lot of pollutants.
    • Oceans and seas: Oceans contain over 97 percent of the water on the planet. However, this water is too salty to drink, cultivate, or irrigate.
    • River and lakes: Rainwater that falls on the earth’s surface is transmitted down the slope of the ground into rivers and streams. Water from melting glaciers on the mountains often falls into some rivers.
    • Groundwater: A portion of the rain seeps through a layer of the earth when it rains. This water eventually reaches solid rocks and collects as groundwater. Drilling wells or sinking tube wells can also attain the water table and acquire groundwater.

Water Use

To say that water is available implies that there is a demand for water. People use water in a variety of ways, extracting it from rivers, lakes, and groundwater for uses such as irrigation, domestic needs, manufacturing, and thermoelectric power production, and also using it in-stream for activities such as transportation, support of fisheries, and hydroelectric power production. Gleick 2014 complies statistics about water use and includes essays on timely water management issues. Molden and Sakthivadivel 1999 introduces a water balance approach for tracking water use and provides useful definitions. Water can often be reused, so using a water resource does not necessarily mean it is no longer available. Perry, et al. 2009 clarifies the distinction between water withdrawals (i.e., the amount of water removed from a resource) and water consumption (i.e., the amount of that water that is transferred to the atmosphere and thus cannot be immediately used again). This is particularly relevant in agriculture, which they focus on, because agriculture is the sector that consumes the most water. De Fraiture and Wichelns 2010 also focuses on agriculture and efficiency, evaluating future irrigation demand and the way efficiency may or may not help sate that demand. Water withdrawals and consumption for domestic and municipal use are much smaller than for agriculture, but these uses are important for both human rights and economic reasons. Flörke, et al. 2013 quantifies the volume of water consumed in households and by industry globally. Padowski and Jawitz 2012 focuses on cities, evaluating both demand and identifying sources of supply that may reach beyond a local watershed due to water transfers. Srinivasan, et al. 2010 evaluates water demand and supply from urban actors other than those directly connected to a municipal water system.

Water Consumption

Water is required for the survival of all living species. On the other hand, humans rely on water more than plants and animals. We use water for a variety of uses in our daily activities, including:

  • Domestic uses: Water is required for drinking, cleaning, cooking, including washing, among other things. Each individual in the house uses an average of 260 litres of water each day for various activities.
  • Industrial uses: Water is used extensively in our industries at many phases of production, from using water as a raw material to the generation of energy. The usage differs per industry.

Governance

The way that people acquire access to water varies across societies and time. Franks and Cleaver 2007 defines governance, relying on theory and illustrating key concepts with a case study. Tropp 2007 provides a historical perspective on efforts to improve access to water, transitioning from a focus almost solely on water infrastructure and technology to a focus on governance. Schmidt and Peppard 2014 reviews how access to water has been conceptualized historically. Dellapenna and Gupta 2008 focuses on water law, providing quite a bit of history. Mukherji and Shah 2005 focuses specifically on groundwater, which tends to have quite different governance because it is distributed and not an integrated system. Ray 2007 evaluates women’s access to water with a focus on how gender-neutral water-development policies have frequently had unintended negative impacts on women. Crow and Sultana 2002 also focuses on women’s access to water and extends the discussion to how women’s access varies according to their social class; an excellent case study from Bangladesh is provided.

Why does water availability matter?

water is vital for agriculture, human consumption, industry, and energy generation. If local surface water and groundwater are used up or contaminated beyond use, it becomes necessary to turn to alternative, often more expensive water sources.

What is Water Scarcity??

Although the definition of water scarcity may at first appear obvious (i.e., the lack of sufficient water to meet demand) in practice the concept is complex. Savenije 2000 lays out the issues clearly and thoroughly, highlighting in particular that water demand by food production dominates the amount of water a person needs. Ohlsson and Turton 1999 describes the interwoven social challenges of reduced water resources followed by adaptation to the new state. Srinivasan, et al. 2012 evaluates water scarcity as a combined function of water supply and demand, governance, and infrastructure. Garrick and Hall 2014 takes a risk-analysis perspective on water scarcity and focuses on built infrastructure. Postel 2000 provides many examples of societies facing the challenges of water limitation. Linton 2004 analyzes the water scarcity literature itself, deconstructing the evolution of the discourse on the global water crisis. Rijsberman 2006 subscribes to the idea that a water crisis came about only after it could be quantified. He provides an excellent critique of water scarcity indicators. Brown and Matlock 2011 provides a thorough overview of existing water scarcity indicators and the range of thresholds they use to demarcate scarcity
